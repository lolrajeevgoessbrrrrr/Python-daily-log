Prompt #7 — Persona / Character
Prompt:

Act as a [input persona/role, e.g. veteran trial lawyer, master sommelier, senior software architect] with [input years of experience] years of experience and a deep command of [input domain/field]. Speak and reason the way someone in that role genuinely would — using their characteristic vocabulary, instincts, and priorities. Your task is to [input task, e.g. explain, advise, critique, debate, mentor] [input subject/topic] for an audience of [input audience, e.g. complete beginners, fellow experts, a nervous client, a curious child]. Your tone should be [input tone, e.g. warm and patient / blunt and no-nonsense / witty and energetic / formal and precise]. Stay fully in character throughout — do not break persona to add disclaimers or hedge like a generic assistant unless safety genuinely requires it. If a term would be unfamiliar to [input audience], briefly define it in plain language before continuing.

What it does well:
It locks in a consistent voice, expertise level, and point of view across the whole response, so explanations feel like they're coming from someone who actually lives in that world rather than a textbook summary. It also lets you tune two things at once — how expert the speaker is and how much the audience already knows — just by adjusting the bracketed inputs.

When to use it:
Use it for mentorship-style explanations, mock interviews, character-driven storytelling, brand-voice copy, simulated expert consultations, or any time you want depth and personality rather than a flat, neutral answer.

When NOT to use it:
Don't use it when you need a strictly neutral, hedge-everything factual answer, or for medical, legal, or financial decisions where a confident "expert voice" could be mistaken for real professional advice. Also avoid it for trivial one-line questions — the persona setup adds overhead with no real payoff.


Prompt #8 — Chain-of-Thought Reasoning
Prompt:

You are solving the following problem: [input problem or question]. This falls under the domain of [input domain, e.g. math, logic, business strategy, code debugging]. Think through it step by step, numbering each step, showing your intermediate reasoning, calculations, or assumptions explicitly before moving to the next step. Do not skip steps even if they seem obvious. Once your reasoning is complete, clearly mark your conclusion with the label "Final Answer:" and state it in [input desired format, e.g. one sentence, a single number, a short paragraph]. If at any point an assumption is required because information is missing, state the assumption clearly rather than guessing silently.

What it does well:
It forces reasoning to happen out loud rather than in one leap, which sharply cuts down on careless errors in multi-step problems and makes every intermediate step easy to check, audit, or correct. It's especially good at catching the kind of mistake that hides inside a skipped step.

When to use it:
Use it for multi-step math, logic puzzles, debugging, multi-constraint planning or scheduling, or any decision that depends on several smaller judgments chained together.

When NOT to use it:
Don't use it for simple factual lookups or single-step questions — it just adds length without adding accuracy. Also avoid it when you need a short, immediately usable final answer with no scaffolding; in that case, pair it with an explicit instruction to show reasoning separately from the final answer, or skip the technique entirely.


Prompt #9 — JSON Output
Prompt:

Extract the following information from the text below: [input fields to extract, e.g. name, sentiment, date, key complaints]. Return ONLY valid JSON, with no preamble, explanation, or markdown formatting, matching this exact schema: [input JSON schema, e.g. {"field1": string, "field2": "optionA" | "optionB", "field3": number}]. The source text is: [input source text/data]. If a field cannot be found in the text, return it as null rather than guessing or omitting it.

What it does well:
It produces clean, machine-readable output that can be fed straight into another script, database, or app without any text-scraping or guesswork, and the strict schema keeps field names, types, and value ranges consistent across hundreds of runs.

When to use it:
Use it for data extraction, batch classification or labeling, populating databases, or anywhere the output needs to plug directly into automated systems rather than be read by a person.

When NOT to use it:
Don't use it for conversational, creative, or exploratory writing — forcing prose into rigid fields strips out nuance and flow. Also avoid it when the output shape is genuinely unpredictable; forcing an unfamiliar fit can make the model fabricate or truncate fields just to satisfy the schema.
